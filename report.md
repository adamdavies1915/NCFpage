# A grassroots playbook for fixing the Norman C. Francis Parkway path

**The neutral ground path on Norman C. Francis Parkway — one of New Orleans' two most heavily used off-street trails — has no active plan for repair or resurfacing, despite forming the critical north-south spine of the city's cycling network.** This is both a problem and an opportunity. The path falls through a jurisdictional crack between the Department of Public Works (paved surfaces) and Parks & Parkways (landscaping), and the recently approved $510 million bond package contains no dedicated line item for it. But a confluence of factors — a new mayor and council, the recent passing of Dr. Norman C. Francis himself, fresh bond money, and completed proof-of-concept projects on the Lafitte Greenway — make this the most favorable political moment in years to push for a fix. This playbook provides the institutional knowledge and campaign strategy to make it happen.

---

## The political landscape you're working with

The Norman C. Francis Parkway runs roughly 27 blocks from the head of Bayou St. John (where it connects to the Lafitte Greenway via a bicycle/pedestrian roundabout) southwest through Mid-City, across Canal Street and the Pontchartrain Expressway, to Martin Luther King Jr. Boulevard in Gert Town. The off-street path on its neutral ground is classified as a shared-use trail on the city's official bike map and is recognized in the **Complete Streets Annual Report 2020–2023** as one of only two "most frequently used off-street trails" in New Orleans, alongside the Lafitte Greenway itself.

**Jurisdiction is split.** The paved path surface falls under the **Department of Public Works (DPW)**, which maintains the city's 1,547 miles of roadway and all hardscape in the public right-of-way. The surrounding grass, trees, and landscaping fall under **Parks & Parkways**. Neither department has clear ownership of the path as a *trail asset* — it sits in an administrative gray zone between road maintenance and park maintenance. This ambiguity is itself a key obstacle your campaign must resolve. The Complete Streets program is jointly administered by DPW and the **Office of Resilience and Sustainability**, giving you a third institutional entry point.

**The corridor spans two (possibly three) City Council districts.** The northern section near Bayou St. John falls in **District D**, represented by **Eugene Green** (504-658-1040). The central and southern sections — from roughly Tulane Avenue through Canal Street and down to MLK Boulevard — fall primarily in **District B**, represented by **Lesli Harris** (504-658-1020). A segment may also touch **District A** (Aimee McCarron, 504-658-1010). The at-large members are **JP Morrell** and **Matthew Willard**. All took office January 12, 2026, under new **Mayor Helena Moreno**. This is a fresh administration eager to establish its priorities — a prime window for advocacy.

Dr. Norman C. Francis died on **February 18, 2026**, at age 94. The outpouring of tributes from Mayor Moreno, former Mayor Landrieu, and council members has been extraordinary. Francis was described as "one of New Orleans' greatest sons" — the longest-serving university president in U.S. history, a Presidential Medal of Freedom recipient, and a civil rights pioneer who housed Freedom Riders. **The path bearing his name is now a living memorial. Making it worthy of that legacy is your campaign's most powerful frame.**

And there is a direct, personal connection between Dr. Francis's story and the very purpose of the path. Dr. Francis was born in Lafayette, Louisiana, the son of poor parents, neither of whom had finished high school. **His father, Joseph Francis, was a barber who rode to work each day on a bicycle because the family could not afford a car.** Young Norman shined shoes on Lafayette's main street to earn pocket money. From these humble beginnings — a working-class Black family in the segregated South, dependent on a bicycle for basic transportation — came one of the most consequential figures in New Orleans history. This detail, documented in multiple biographical accounts including a Washington Informer profile and Wikipedia, provides an extraordinarily resonant narrative thread connecting Dr. Francis's origins to the people who use the path that bears his name today.

## How New Orleans funds infrastructure — and where the money is

Understanding the funding landscape is essential because path resurfacing is not free, but it is relatively cheap compared to most infrastructure projects. A 27-block neutral ground path resurfacing likely costs in the low single-digit millions — modest by city capital standards.

**The $510 million bond package** approved by voters in November 2025 with 77% support is the most immediate funding source. While there is no dedicated line item for the NCF Parkway path, several bond categories could cover it. The **"Citywide Greenspace Restoration"** project under Parks & Parkways explicitly includes "replacement of site infrastructure (benches, walkways, fencing, drinking fountains, signage, trash receptacles, and lighting)" in "neutral grounds" citywide. The **Lafitte Greenway expansion** ($4.8 million for a half-mile extension) demonstrates bond money flowing to trail infrastructure. Your campaign's first concrete ask should be **getting path resurfacing included in the bond spending plan** as it is allocated by the Moreno administration and council.

**Other funding streams to target:**

- **Fair Share revenue** delivers approximately **$5.7 million annually to DPW** from tourism taxes. DPW uses this for maintenance, but its budget was cut ~40% in 2025, creating a backlog.
- **Transportation Alternatives Program (TAP)** funding, administered by the **New Orleans Regional Planning Commission (RPC)** as the Metropolitan Planning Organization, provides 80/20 federal/local match grants for bike/pedestrian facilities. Projects must be included in the **Metropolitan Transportation Plan (MTP)** and the **Transportation Improvement Program (TIP)** to receive federal funds. Getting the NCF Parkway path into the TIP is a medium-term goal.
- **EPA Climate Pollution Reduction Grant**: New Orleans already received **$50 million** including $10.7 million for 7 new miles of protected bikeways. Your path may qualify for inclusion in this spending.
- **Surface Transportation Block Grant Program (STBG)** and **Safe Streets and Roads for All (SS4A)** grants are competitive federal programs that fund bike/pedestrian infrastructure, though the current federal administration has shown hostility toward cycling-specific grants.
- **State Recreational Trails Program** funded the original Lafitte Greenway construction and could fund path rehabilitation.

**A critical political reality:** Federal cycling infrastructure funding is under threat. In September 2025, USDOT began rescinding or withholding previously awarded grants for bike lanes and trails. The Transportation Alternatives Program survived but faces uncertainty. This makes **city and state funding sources more important than ever** — and gives your campaign urgency.

## Your campaign in five phases

### Phase 1: Document, organize, and define the ask (Weeks 1–6)

**Document the problem systematically.** Walk or ride every block of the neutral ground path from Bayou St. John to MLK Boulevard. Photograph and GPS-tag every crack, heave, root intrusion, gap, and hazard. Create a simple visual report — a map overlaid with condition photos — that makes the problem undeniable. Note ADA compliance issues (uneven surfaces, missing curb cuts, obstructed access) because ADA violations create legal obligations the city cannot ignore.

**Launch a coordinated 311 campaign.** File individual NOLA 311 requests (via nola311.org, the app, or by calling 504-539-3266) for every distinct surface defect. Categorize them as **pothole/road surface issues** (routed to DPW Maintenance Division) rather than park complaints (routed to Parks & Parkways). Include photos with every request. The DPW Maintenance Dashboard, created through a 2023 council ordinance, tracks 311 requests publicly — your filings will create a visible data trail. Recruit 20–30 regular path users to file their own reports about the same issues from different angles and using different complaint categories. This volume creates institutional pressure and legal documentation. When requests are closed without resolution, **refile them immediately** noting the original reference number.

**Build your core team.** Recruit 8–12 committed people representing different constituencies: daily bike commuters, joggers, parents with strollers, wheelchair users, neighborhood association leaders from Mid-City and Gert Town, and at least one business owner along the corridor (the Crescent City Farmers Market at 436 N. Norman C. Francis Parkway, which operates every Thursday at the Greenway intersection, is a natural ally). Give the campaign a name that emphasizes the legacy angle — something like "A Path Worthy of Francis" or "Fix the Francis Trail."

**Define your ask precisely.** Based on your documentation, specify exactly what you want: full resurfacing of the neutral ground path from Bayou St. John to MLK Boulevard with smooth asphalt (matching the Lafitte Greenway's 12-foot-wide standard), including ADA-compliant crossings at every intersection, proper drainage, and wayfinding signage connecting to the Greenway. Produce a one-page brief with a rough cost estimate. A 1.5-mile path resurfacing at 12 feet wide likely costs **$1–3 million** — well within the scale of bond-funded projects already approved.

### Phase 2: Build your coalition and establish credibility (Weeks 4–12)

**Align with established organizations.** Contact **Bike Easy** (the city's primary cycling advocacy nonprofit, led by Executive Director Allene La Spina) and the **Lafitte Greenway Partnership** (Executive Director Jason Neville). Both organizations already recognize the NCF Parkway path as a critical connector. Bike Easy leads the **New Orleans Complete Streets Coalition** of ~30 member organizations — getting a resolution of support from this coalition gives you institutional weight. The **UNO Transportation Institute** (researcher Tara Tolford) has permanent automated bike/pedestrian counters on the NCF Parkway trail through the **Count Dat** program and can provide usage data to support your case.

**Engage neighborhood associations.** Present at meetings of the **Faubourg St. John Neighborhood Association**, **Mid-City Neighborhood Organization**, and **Gert Town Revival Initiative** (or equivalent). Frame the path not as a cyclist issue but as a **neighborhood connectivity and safety asset** — linking residents to the Lafitte Greenway, Bayou St. John, the farmers market, schools, and Xavier University. Collect letters of support. The lesson from the **Algiers bike lane removal** (September 2022, when the council voted 7-0 to remove 2.2 miles of protected bike lanes after residents complained about inadequate community engagement) is unambiguous: **broad neighborhood buy-in before political action is non-negotiable.**

**Engage Xavier University.** The parkway runs past Xavier's campus and is named for its legendary president. Xavier's institutional support — a letter from the university president, involvement of students — would be enormously powerful, especially in the weeks following Dr. Francis's passing. The connection runs even deeper than the name: Dr. Francis's own father depended on a bicycle as his daily transportation, riding to his barbershop in Lafayette because the family couldn't afford a car. Xavier students today who cycle to campus along the path are living out the same reality Joseph Francis knew — and the university has every reason to champion infrastructure that serves them.

**Gather your data.** Request usage counts from UNOTI (the COVID-era data showed a **67% increase in cycling** on this trail). Cross-reference with the Complete Streets Annual Report finding that it's one of the city's two most-used off-street trails. Compile crash and near-miss data from the corridor. Note that **53.4% of all roadway fatalities in New Orleans in 2022 were pedestrians and cyclists**, and **72% of roadway fatalities occurred in disadvantaged communities** the parkway serves.

### Phase 3: Engage elected officials and bureaucracy (Weeks 8–16)

**Request meetings with your council members.** Lesli Harris (District B) and Eugene Green (District D) are the primary targets. Bring your documentation, your coalition letters, your usage data, and your one-page ask. Invite each council member to **walk or ride the path with you** — firsthand experience of bumping over cracked asphalt is more persuasive than any report. Make three specific requests:

1. **Immediate**: Direct DPW to conduct a formal pavement condition assessment of the neutral ground path and assign clear departmental ownership (DPW or Parks & Parkways) for trail maintenance.
2. **Near-term**: Allocate bond funds from the "Citywide Greenspace Restoration" category or a council-district infrastructure allocation to fund resurfacing.
3. **Systemic**: Include the NCF Parkway trail in the city's next Capital Improvement Plan cycle and in the RPC's Transportation Improvement Program for federal funding eligibility.

**Engage the new Moreno administration directly.** The Mayor's Office of Transportation (if retained from the Cantrell administration) or the Office of Resilience and Sustainability are your executive branch entry points. Frame the path repair as a **quick, visible, popular win** for a new mayor — far less controversial than on-street bike lanes, with an existing user base and a beloved namesake.

**Attend the right meetings.** The **City Planning Commission** oversees the Five-Year Capital Improvement Plan. The **RPC Transportation Policy Board** programs federal funds. Public comment at these bodies puts your project on the institutional record. Track meeting schedules at council.nola.gov and norpc.org.

**Contact the at-large members.** JP Morrell, the council vice president, sponsored the community engagement ordinance after the Algiers debacle — he cares about process. Matthew Willard covers the whole city. Both can amplify your ask across district lines.

### Phase 4: Media and public pressure (Weeks 10–20)

**Lead with the Francis family story.** Dr. Francis's father, Joseph, rode a bicycle to his barbershop every day because the family couldn't afford a car. His son Norman became one of the most consequential New Orleanians in American history. Today, the path named for Norman — used daily by thousands of people, many of whom still can't afford a car — is falling apart. This narrative arc is your campaign's emotional centerpiece. It connects legacy, equity, and transportation access in a single story that no one can dismiss as a niche cycling issue.

**Write the op-ed.** An op-ed in The Times-Picayune/NOLA.com or The Advocate built around the Joseph Francis bicycle detail will resonate deeply. Have it written not by a cycling advocate but by a **community leader, Xavier alumnus, or civil rights figure** — someone whose voice carries moral authority beyond the bike community. The op-ed should open with Joseph on his bicycle in Lafayette, move through Norman's extraordinary life, and land on the crumbling path that bears his name today.

**Create visual content.** Side-by-side comparisons of the smooth Lafitte Greenway surface and the deteriorated NCF Parkway path surface are your most powerful visual. Before/after renderings showing what a restored path could look like. A short video of a wheelchair user struggling over cracked pavement. Post these on Instagram, Facebook, Twitter/X, and Nextdoor (where neighborhood-level infrastructure conversations happen).

**Pitch local media.** The Mid-City Messenger covers the corridor closely. WWNO (public radio) and Verite News do strong infrastructure reporting. WDSU and WWL-TV will cover a community event. Your hooks are: the Francis family bicycle story, the equity angle (the path connects disadvantaged communities to jobs and services), the safety data (cyclist and pedestrian fatalities at crisis levels), and the "gap in the network" story (the Greenway is beautiful, the path connecting to it is falling apart).

**Host a community ride and walk.** Organize a "Ride for Francis" or "Walk the Path" event from the Lafitte Greenway roundabout to Xavier University, inviting families, seniors, wheelchair users, and Xavier students. Make it a celebration of Dr. Francis's legacy combined with a call to action. Invite council members and media. The Bicycle Second Line tradition (Bike Easy hosts these twice yearly with brass bands and ~500 participants) provides a cultural template.

### Phase 5: Sustained pressure and implementation (Months 5–18)

**Track the bond allocation process.** The $510M in bonds must be allocated to specific projects. The GO Bond Project List is published by the Office of Safety and Emergency Preparedness. Monitor council budget hearings and push for NCF Parkway path resurfacing to be included. The Citywide Greenspace Restoration line item under Parks & Parkways is your best existing vehicle — it explicitly covers "walkways" on "neutral grounds."

**Push for inclusion in the Transportation Improvement Program.** Work with the RPC (the MPO for greater New Orleans) to get the path listed in the Metropolitan Transportation Plan and TIP, making it eligible for federal Transportation Alternatives funding (80% federal / 20% local match). This is a 6–12 month process but positions the project for federal dollars.

**Maintain 311 pressure.** Continue coordinated filing. Track resolution rates on the DPW Maintenance Dashboard. Publicize the city's response (or non-response) — the Office of Inspector General has already flagged problems with 311 pothole repair transparency.

**Celebrate incremental wins.** If the city patches a section, publicize it and thank officials by name (including via 311 — positive feedback reinforces good decisions). If they commit to a study, celebrate it. Each win builds momentum toward the full resurfacing.

## How to frame the issue for maximum support

The Algiers experience proved that framing a project as "for cyclists" triggers opposition. Your campaign must frame the NCF Parkway path as something larger. Here are the frames that work, in order of likely local resonance:

**Legacy, honor, and a family bicycle.** Dr. Francis's father, Joseph Francis, was a barber in Lafayette who rode to work each day on a bicycle because the family could not afford a car. Young Norman shined shoes on Lafayette's main street to earn pocket money. From these humble beginnings — a working-class Black family in the segregated South, dependent on a bicycle for basic transportation — came one of the most consequential New Orleanians in American history: a Presidential Medal of Freedom recipient, the longest-serving university president in the country, and a man who advised eight U.S. presidents.

Today, roughly 20% of New Orleans households still lack access to a vehicle. The path bearing Norman Francis's name is used daily by thousands of people — many of whom, like Joseph Francis, depend on a bicycle not for recreation but to get to work, school, and the grocery store. That path is crumbling. The generational arc from Joseph Francis's bicycle commute to the current state of the trail named for his son is your campaign's most powerful narrative. It connects legacy, equity, and transportation access in a single story that transcends cycling politics entirely and resonates with every constituency — especially in the immediate aftermath of Dr. Francis's passing.

**Safety and equity.** New Orleans has one of the highest rates of cyclist and pedestrian fatalities among major U.S. metros. Traffic fatalities increased **57% from 2017 to 2021**, and **72% occurred in disadvantaged communities**. Roughly **20% of New Orleans households lack vehicle access** — for these residents, a safe path isn't recreation, it's transportation. The path connects Gert Town (a historically underinvested Black neighborhood) to Mid-City, the Lafitte Greenway, Bayou St. John, and City Park.

**Network connectivity.** A smooth NCF Parkway path combined with the Lafitte Greenway creates a continuous off-street corridor from Uptown/Gert Town to the French Quarter — the backbone of the city's 120+ mile bike network. Bike Easy already describes this connection as the primary route from Uptown to Mid-City. A broken link in the backbone degrades the entire network.

**Economic development.** The Crescent City Farmers Market (150,000+ shoppers annually) operates at the NCF Parkway/Greenway intersection. The former Lindy Boggs Medical Center at NCF Parkway and Bienville is a $100M+ mixed-use redevelopment project. Nationally, properties near quality trails sell for **11% more** (Indianapolis Monon Trail data), and protected bike lanes correlate with **8–12% sales increases** for adjacent businesses.

**ADA accessibility.** A crumbling path is not just inconvenient — it's a barrier for wheelchair users, parents with strollers, and anyone with mobility challenges. ADA compliance is a legal requirement, not optional. This frame broadens your coalition beyond cyclists to the disability community.

## What success looks like — and a realistic timeline

**Quick wins (3–6 months):** DPW conducts a formal pavement assessment and assigns clear departmental ownership for the trail. Emergency patching of the worst hazards. Council members publicly commit to pursuing funding.

**Medium-term victory (6–18 months):** Bond funds allocated for full path resurfacing. Design work begins. Project included in the next Capital Improvement Plan.

**Full success (18–36 months):** The Norman C. Francis Parkway neutral ground path is resurfaced to a smooth, 12-foot-wide asphalt standard matching the Lafitte Greenway, with ADA-compliant crossings, proper drainage, wayfinding signage, and a clear maintenance plan with assigned departmental responsibility.

The infrastructure is there. The community uses it. The political moment — a new administration, fresh bond money, and a city mourning the man whose name is on the street — is as favorable as it will ever be. The path just needs someone to champion it. That's your campaign.
